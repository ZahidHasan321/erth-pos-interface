# Questions for the shop (ERTH)

A set of questions about how the shop side should work, mostly the feedback (brova trial) flow plus stocktake. For each one we say what the system does today, give a quick scenario where it helps, then ask. Once you answer, we set it as the agreed way of working and drop the question from this list.

---

## 1. Who takes the feedback vs who takes the money

**What we have now.** There are two separate parts of the system. One is the showroom side, where the brova trial feedback is taken. The other is the cashier side, which handles payments and the handover, and only the cashier can get into it. Nothing forces the same person or two different people to do these, one person could do both, or two people could split them.

**Scenario.** A customer finishes trying on their brova and there is still a balance to settle.

**Questions.**

1. Is the person who sat with the customer and took the trial feedback the same person who then takes the money, or two different people at two desks? Does it depend on the shop (small branch one person does everything, busy branch a separate cashier)?
2. Should the system leave this open so anyone can do either, or do you want each task tied to a specific role?

---

## 2. Editing a feedback after it is submitted

**What we have now.** A brova trial's feedback can be re-opened and edited after it is first saved (the verdict, the measurements, the notes). A truly new feedback only starts when the thobe physically comes back from the workshop for another alteration. There is no lock, in practice it stays editable.

**Scenario.** Staff record "accept with fix" on a brova, the customer leaves, the thobe goes to the workshop, and an hour later they realise they picked the wrong thing.

**Questions.**

1. Should a submitted feedback stay editable, or should it lock?
2. If it locks, at what moment, the instant it is submitted, or once the thobe is sent back to the workshop?
3. If it can still be edited, can anyone do it or only a manager?

Worth keeping in mind, if feedback stays open to edit, staff can quickly fix an honest mistake, but it also means what the customer agreed to could be changed later without anyone noticing. If it locks, an honest mistake then needs someone to reopen it.

---

## 3. Does the customer go to the cashier after the trial

**What we have now.** At the trial the customer can change the style. The thobe is updated to the new style, but the system does not work out the new price by itself, that is left to the cashier when the customer pays. For now it only shows a reminder that there is a balance to settle.

**Scenario.** At the trial the customer upgrades to a fancier collar that costs more.

**Questions.**

1. After a trial where the price went up, does the customer go to the cashier right then to pay the difference, or only later at final pickup?
2. If a change makes the order cheaper, do they get the difference back at that moment or at pickup?
3. For a trial where the price does not change at all (just a small fit fix), do they need to see the cashier at all, or do they just leave and come back when it is ready?

Worth keeping in mind, if a price difference is only settled at final pickup, it is easy for it to be forgotten or argued about later. If it is settled right at the trial, it is one extra step at the cashier while the customer is still in the shop.

---

## 4. Rejecting a brova as "redo", what are we waiting for

**What we have now.** A "redo" means the design itself is wrong, not a small fix. The rejected brova is discarded and a fresh replacement is made from scratch. The system already handles the new fabric: our own stock fabric is cut fresh straight away, customer-brought fabric parks until the customer brings more, and if our fabric is short it parks for a manager (reorder, substitute, consult, or refund).

**Scenario.** The customer rejects a brova as redo.

**Questions.**

1. For each of these, tell us if it is a real case and what should happen, and add any we missed:
   - the customer brought their own fabric, so we wait for them to bring more
   - we give them a replacement fabric from our stock and start right away
   - instead of making a fresh trial, we make one corrected thobe first, bring it back for the customer to try, and only make the rest once they approve it
   - we skip the trial completely, start the finals straight into production, and refund the customer for that brova
   - anything else
2. When a redo happens, does the customer pay anything extra, get a refund, or does nothing change money-wise?
3. Does the customer have to be there and agree before we start, or can staff start it after they have left?

Worth keeping in mind, if we skip the trial and make the finals straight away, the customer never approves the new design before it is made, so if it still is not right we are back to redoing all of them.

---

## 5. The photos, voice notes, signature and notes from the trial

**What we have now.** During the trial staff can capture photos and videos, voice notes in the customer's words, a signature, and typed notes. All of it is saved with that trial and you can see it again later on that thobe in the showroom. It does not get sent anywhere by itself, the workshop does not get its own view of it tied to the fix it is about to do.

**Scenario.** The customer says the chest is a bit tight, so staff record a voice note ("chest looser by two fingers"), snap a photo of the fit, get his signature, and jot a note, and then the thobe goes back to the workshop for the fix.

**Questions.**

1. The photos, voice notes and written notes, who are they really for, the workshop to actually see and hear what to fix, a record in case of a dispute, or just a showroom memory aid? And should they travel to the workshop with the thobe, or stay showroom-only?
2. The signature, what does it stand for (the customer accepted the trial, approved a price change, collected the order), and is it required or optional?

---

## 6. The order of the measurement list

**What we have now.** At the trial, the measurements are shown in a fixed order, grouped as collar and length and chest, then pockets, then waist and arms and bottom, then jabzour, then basma. It is roughly top of the body downward, but it was not arranged around how a tailor actually re-measures someone.

**Questions.**

1. Is there a specific order you go through the measurements in when you check them on the customer (like strictly head to toe) that the list should follow, so you can go straight down it without jumping around?
2. Or should the ones that usually need fixing sit at the top?
3. Or is the current order fine?

---

## 7. Several brovas with the same measurement

**What we have now.** Each brova gets its own feedback, there is no copy-to-all that fans one verdict across the others. For the finals waiting on the brovas, staff already resolve each final on its own (adopt the corrected measurement or keep the old one, match the brova's style or customise it). So feedback is per-brova today, even when two brovas share the same measurement.

**Scenario.** A customer orders 3 thobes with the same measurement, but one has a different collar than the others.

**Questions.**

1. When brovas share the same measurement, do you want to record the fit once for all of them, but keep the style feedback separate per brova because the collars differ?
2. If both the measurement and the style are the same across the brovas, should it just be one feedback for all of them?
3. Or do you want it kept separate per brova anyway, since each one is tried on by itself?
4. In practice, does the customer try on every brova, or just one and assume the rest match?

---

## 8. Who hands the finished order over

**What we have now.** The finished thobes do not get a feedback form, they are simply collected. The handover happens on the cashier side, the cashier marks each thobe collected or delivered. Pickup is not blocked on payment, it is staff judgment. For home delivery the cashier marks it delivered at the counter, there is no separate driver step today.

**Scenario.** The order is finished and the customer comes in to collect, maybe still owing part of the balance.

**Questions.**

1. Who actually hands the thobes over at pickup, the cashier, the showroom person, or whoever is free?
2. For home delivery, who marks it handed to the driver or delivered, the cashier at the counter, or should there be a separate driver or confirmation step before it counts as delivered?
3. Can a customer take some of their thobes and leave the rest, and who decides that is allowed?
4. Does whoever hands it over deal with any leftover payment, or is that fully the cashier's job?

---

## 9. The happy or unhappy face rating

**What we have now.** At the trial we capture a happy or unhappy face on a 1 to 5 scale. Today it is just stored, nothing reads it or shows it anywhere.

**Scenario.** A customer leaves the trial clearly unhappy.

**Questions.**

1. Do you want this rating used for something, like flagging that unhappy customer for a manager to follow up, or tracking how happy customers are over time?
2. Or is just capturing it enough?

---

## 10. One brova accepted, another rejected

**What we have now.** As soon as any one brova is accepted, all the parked finals are released and start production. If another brova on the same order was rejected, the rejected one is handled separately (repaired or redone) while the finals carry on.

**Scenario.** A customer accepts one brova but rejects another.

**Questions.**

1. Is releasing the finals right away (because one brova was accepted) what you want, or should everything wait until all the brovas are accepted?

Worth keeping in mind, releasing the finals as soon as one brova is accepted saves time, but if a later brova turns out to need a redo on something the finals share, some of the finals may already be in production.

---

## 11. Letting the showroom flag an investigation

**What we have now.** The workshop already opens an investigation automatically when a thobe has come back too many times, so a manager looks into it. On the shop side there is a checkbox to open an investigation too, but at the moment it does not actually do anything.

**Scenario.** A thobe keeps coming back and the showroom staff can tell something is genuinely wrong with it, not just a normal alteration.

**Questions.**

1. Should the showroom be able to open an investigation too, so a manager looks into the thobe?
2. If yes, should opening it just notify the manager, or also hold the thobe until the manager decides?

---

## 12. Tracking customer-brought fabric

**What we have now.** When a customer brings their own fabric we only write down a description of it (colour, where it is from, length). It is never tracked as stock. For a redo or alteration on customer fabric we just park "customer must bring more".

**Scenario.** A customer brings their own bolt of fabric, we cut the thobe from it, and there is some left over.

**Questions.**

1. Do you want to actually track how much of the customer's fabric we are holding and how much is left over?
2. Or is just writing down the description fine?

---

## 13. Stocktake

**What we have now.** The shop counts its own stock (fabrics, shelf items, accessories) at least once a month. You open a stocktake, the system lists every item, you type in what you actually counted, it shows you the difference from what it expected, and for anything that does not match you give a reason, then a manager checks and confirms it, and it is saved in history. If you go over a month without counting you get warnings that get more insistent after a few days, but nothing is ever locked.

**Questions.**

1. Is this overall flow right, or is it missing a step?
2. Is once a month right, or should fast-moving things be counted more often, or counted by section on a rotation?
3. Do you count everything in one go, or a section at a time (fabrics one day, shelf another)?
4. When something does not match, do you just fix the number, or do you investigate where it went, and should a mismatch get logged as waste?
5. Should every mismatch need a manager to confirm, or can a trusted person fix small ones themselves and only big ones go to a manager?
6. Who does the counting, one person, or two people checking each other?
7. Should being overdue ever actually block work, or is the warning-only approach fine?
